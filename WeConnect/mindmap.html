TOGAF Standard Mind Map (Textual Representation)

  • The TOGAF Standard
    • An open, industry consensus framework for Enterprise Architecture.
    • Provides a standard approach for developing, approving, using, and maintaining Enterprise Architectures.
    • Applies to all Enterprise Architecture practices.
    • Based on an iterative process model supported by best practices and re-usable assets.
    • Developed and maintained by The Open Group Architecture Forum.
    • Purpose: To guide effective change within an enterprise. Supports strategy, portfolio, project, or solution delivery.
  • TOGAF Documentation Set Structure
    • Structured to address transition from universal concepts to unique configurations.
    • Includes the formal **TOGAF Standard** and the broader **TOGAF Library**.
    • Modular structure with a hierarchy.
    • Intent: Allows specialization and isolation of detailed areas.
    • The TOGAF Standard
      • Represents proven, stable, scalable best practices and concepts.
      • Consists of:
        • TOGAF Fundamental Content: Essential concepts and established best practices that are stable and enduring. Universally applicable. (See below for details)
        • TOGAF Series Guides: Build upon Fundamental Content. Provide extended guidance for specific topics, concerns, and use-cases. Proven, stable best practices. (See below for examples)
    • The TOGAF Library
      • Contains additional resources accompanying the standard.
      • Includes emerging ideas, guidelines, templates, patterns, and reference material.
      • Purpose: Accelerate creation of new architectures.
      • Material may include reference architectures developed by industry experts (e.g., IT4IT, Commercial Aviation, O-PAS).
      • Documents can be used as-is or as a starting point for organizational EA.
  • TOGAF Fundamental Content
    • Consists of six documents.
    • Introduction and Core Concepts
      • Provides executive overview, documentation structure, core concepts, and terminology.
      • Core Concepts mentioned include: ADM, EA Services, Deliverables/Artifacts/Building Blocks, Architecture Principles, Enterprise Continuum, Architecture Repository, EA Capability and Governance, Architecture Views/Viewpoints, Enterprise Agility, Risk Management.
    • The Architecture Development Method (ADM)
      • **Central to the TOGAF Framework**.
      • A tested and repeatable process for developing architectures.
      • An iterative approach to developing an Enterprise Architecture.
      • Designed to address business requirements.
      • Includes establishing framework, developing content, transitioning, and governing realization.
      • Driven by the Requirements Management process.
      • **Phases**:
        • Preliminary Phase: Preparation and initiation activities for establishing an Architecture Capability, including framework tailoring and principles definition.
        • Requirements Management: Process for managing architecture requirements throughout the ADM.
        • Phase A: Architecture Vision: Initial phase, defining scope, identifying stakeholders, creating Architecture Vision, obtaining approval.
        • Phase B: Business Architecture: Describes development of Business Architecture to support Architecture Vision.
        • Phase C: Information Systems Architectures: Describes development of Data and Application Architectures.
        • Phase D: Technology Architecture: Describes development of Technology Architecture.
        • Phase E: Opportunities and Solutions: Identifying delivery vehicles (projects, programs) and initial implementation planning.
        • Phase F: Migration Planning: Finalizing Implementation and Migration Plan from Baseline to Target Architectures.
        • Phase G: Implementation Governance: Providing architectural oversight for implementation projects, ensuring conformance.
        • Phase H: Architecture Change Management: Establishing procedures for managing change to the architecture.
      • ADM Techniques
        • Collection of techniques for applying the TOGAF approach and ADM.
        • Stakeholder Management: Discipline to win support, identify players, used throughout phases.
        • Gap Analysis: Technique to highlight shortfalls between Baseline and Target Architectures, typically final step in a phase.
        • Migration Planning Techniques: Support planning in Phases E/F. Includes Implementation Factor Catalog, Consolidated Gaps, Solutions, and Dependencies Matrix, Architecture Definition Increments Table, Transition Architecture State Evolution Table, Business Value Assessment Matrix.
        • Risk Management: Technique used to mitigate risk during architecture/transformation projects. Involves classification, identification, assessment, mitigation, monitoring.
        • Architecture Alternatives and Trade-Offs: Technique to identify alternative Target Architectures and understand competing forces.
      • Applying the ADM
        • Guidelines for adapting the ADM to various usage scenarios.
        • Architecture Styles: Adapting framework to different architectural styles (focus, form, techniques, etc.).
        • Iteration and the ADM: ADM supports various iteration concepts (Landscape, Cycle, Capability). Iterations can group related activities.
        • Applying the ADM Across the Architecture Landscape: Using levels of detail to manage complexity (Strategic, Segment, Capability).
        • Architecture Partitioning: Used to simplify development and management, reflecting organizational model.
    • Architecture Content
      • Describes the TOGAF Content Framework (metamodel) and typical deliverables/artifacts/ABBs.
      • **Content Framework**: Detailed model of architectural work products to drive consistency, provide checklist, reduce risk, mandate standards.
      • **Architectural Work Products**: Categories used to classify products.
        • Deliverable: Contractually specified work product, output of projects.
        • Artifact: Architectural work product describing an aspect. Classified as catalogs, matrices, diagrams. Form content of Repository.
        • Architecture Building Block (ABB): Potentially re-usable component capturing architecture requirements, directs SBB development/procurement.
        • Solution Building Block (SBB): Implementation choice, conforms to ABB specification, defines implementation.
      • Architecture Repository
        • Provides a structural framework for managing and leveraging architectural work products.
        • Central place for storing/accessing architectural assets.
        • Part of the wider Enterprise Repository.
      • **The Enterprise Continuum**: Categorization mechanism for artifacts as they evolve from generic Foundation Architectures to Organization-Specific Architectures. Comprises Architecture Continuum and Solutions Continuum.
    • Enterprise Architecture Capability and Governance
      • Resources, guidelines, templates for establishing architecture practice and governance.
      • Describes organization, processes, skills, roles, responsibilities for EA function.
      • **Architecture Capability**: Ability to develop, use, maintain architecture and govern change. Need differs per organization. Has broad purposes (Support Strategy, Portfolio, Project, Solution Delivery). Consists of foundational and purpose-specific activities.
      • **Architecture Governance**: Practice of managing and controlling Enterprise Architectures enterprise-wide. Ensures compliance with standards/regulations. Establishes decision structures and accountability.
      • **Architecture Board**: Decision-making framework for architecture. Responsible for operational and governance items. Approves products, resolves conflicts, enforces compliance.
      • **Architecture Contracts**: Joint agreements between development partners and sponsors on deliverables, quality, fitness-for-purpose. Ensure governed approach to implementation.
  • ADM Deliverables (Typical Baseline)
    • Work products typically created, used, or enriched in ADM phases.
    • Tailored Architecture Framework (Preliminary Phase output)
    • Organizational Model for Enterprise Architecture (Preliminary Phase output)
    • Architecture Principles (Preliminary Phase output)
    • Request for Architecture Work (Preliminary, Phase H output, ADM input)
    • Statement of Architecture Work (Phase A output, Phase H input)
    • Architecture Vision (Phase A output, Phase E, G, H input/updated)
    • Communications Plan (Phase A output)
    • Capability Assessment (Phase A output, Phase E, G, H input/updated)
    • Architecture Definition Document: Container for core architectural artifacts across domains and states (Phase A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H input/updated)
    • Architecture Requirements Specification: Quantitative statements outlining implementation requirements (Phase B, C, D, E, F, G output/input, Requirements Management input/output)
    • Architecture Roadmap: Lists work packages on a timeline to realize Target Architecture (Phase B, C, D, E, F output/input, Phase G, H input)
    • Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs): Capture requirements, guide development (Phase B, C, D, E, F, G output/input)
    • Solution Building Blocks (SBBs): Implementation choices (Phase E, F, G output/input)
    • Implementation and Migration Plan: Schedule of projects (Phase E, F output/input, Phase G, H input)
    • Transition Architecture: Describes an architecturally significant interim state (Phase E, F output/input)
    • Implementation Governance Model (Phase E, F output, Phase G, H input)
    • Architecture Contracts: Joint agreements on deliverables, quality, fitness-for-purpose (Phase G output, Phase H input)
    • Change Request: Initiates further architecture work due to changes/deviations (Phase G, H output/input)
    • Compliance Assessment: Review of implementation projects for alignment (Phase G output, Phase H input)
    • Requirements Impact Assessment: Assesses impact of new info/changes on requirements (Phase H output, Requirements Management output)
  • TOGAF Series Guides (Domain-Specific Guidance Examples)
    • General How-To Guidance: Practitioners’ Approach, Using the TOGAF Standard in the Digital Enterprise, Digital Technology Adoption.
    • Establishing an Enterprise Architecture Team Guidance: The TOGAF Leader’s Guide to Establishing and Evolving an EA Capability.
    • Security Architecture
      • Integrating Risk and Security within a TOGAF Enterprise Architecture guide.
      • Security is a cross-cutting concern pervasive through EA.
      • Risk is the "effect of uncertainty on objectives".
      • Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) and Information Security Management (ISM) processes underpin Security Architecture.
    • Business Architecture
      • Includes guides on Business Models, Business Capabilities, Value Streams, Information Mapping, Organization Mapping, Business Scenarios.
      • Business Models: Description of rationale for how an organization creates, delivers, and captures value. Starting point for innovation and strategy.
      • **Business Capability**: **What** a business does or can do. A particular ability or capacity. Delineates *what* without explaining *how, why, or where*. Endures over longer planning horizons. Forms Business Capability Model/Map.
      • **Value Stream**: **How** the business delivers value. An end-to-end collection of value-adding activities (stages) that create an overall result for a stakeholder. Describes the *how* value is delivered. Mapping helps assess effectiveness.
      • Mapping: Value Streams can be mapped to Business Capabilities to show how activities utilize abilities.
      • Information Mapping: Articulates, characterizes, and represents information critical to the business. Can be mapped to capabilities, value streams, organizations.
      • Organization Mapping: Shows organizational units, partners, stakeholders, and their working relationships. Provides organizational context, can be mapped to capabilities.
      • Business Scenarios: Technique to understand business requirements and align with IT needs. Describes process, environment, actors, desired outcome.
    • Data/Information Architecture
      • Includes guide on Customer Master Data Management (C-MDM).
      • Purpose of C-MDM: Increase value of customer information shared across the organization.
    • Agile Methods
      • Includes guides on Enabling Enterprise Agility and Applying the TOGAF ADM using Agile Sprints.
      • Enterprise agility characteristics: Responsiveness, value-driven, experimentation, empowered teams, customer collaboration, continuous improvement, respect for people. Important for reacting to change.
      • Different levels of detail (Strategic, Segment, Capability) enable agility.
      • Hierarchy of ADM cycles approach for Agile EA.
      • Intentional Architecture: Planned strategies/initiatives to enhance solution design and provide guidance (often delivered as part of Capability Architecture).
      • Agile Enterprise Architecture Collaboration Approaches: EA Development Agility, Solution Collaboration, Cross-Development Collaboration, Cross-Functional Agility.
    • Reference Models and Method
      • Includes guides on DBRM, GRM, Architecture Maturity Models, Architecture Project Management, Architecture Skills Framework.
      • Digital Business Reference Model (DBRM): Industry-independent outline of core components for digital enterprise. Elements categorized into Digital, Strategic, Structural, Operational domains.
      • Government Reference Model (GRM): Standard template to describe public sector businesses for transformational change. Provides taxonomy and common language.
      • Architecture Maturity Models (CMMs): Techniques for evaluating organizational EA maturity. Provide method to improve change processes.
      • Architecture Project Management: Guidelines for managing Architecture Projects using techniques from PRINCE2/PMBOK alongside ADM. Enhances success via planning, monitoring, communication.
      • Architecture Skills Framework: Set of role, skill, and experience norms for EA work. Addresses lack of uniformity in EA roles/skills. Includes generic, business, EA, PM, IT general, technical IT, legal skills.
  • Supporting the Digital Enterprise
    • Based on the TOGAF Series Guide: Using the TOGAF Standard in the Digital Enterprise. Aligns with the Digital Practitioner Body of Knowledge™ (DPBoK™) Standard.
    • DPBoK Contexts (Emergence Model):
      • Context I: Individual/Founder: Bare minimum requirements of delivering digital value. Key topics: Conception of digital value, digital infrastructure, Agile development/continuous delivery. EA supports by communication, infrastructure description, risk understanding.
      • Context II: Team: Collaborative Product team. Key topics: Product management, work execution management, operations management. EA supports by architecture models, depicting workflows/processes, communication/collaboration efficacy, risk understanding.
      • Context III: Team of Teams: Coordinating across multiple teams. Key topics: Organization/cultural factors, coordination/process mechanisms, investment/portfolio consequences. EA helps resolve cultural issues, depict processes, support portfolio management. Continues to ensure risk is understood and communication is effective.
      • Context IV: Enduring Enterprise: Managing a sustainable business over long periods. Key topics: Governance, risk, security, compliance, information management, architecture/portfolio management. EA expands role into strategy/governance, provides compliance criteria, supports information management, supports portfolio management.
    • Strategies to support digital enterprise success:
      • Peek-Ahead Strategy: Provide just enough guidance (risks, standards, best practices) while looking ahead to the next context transition.
      • Enterprise Architecture as Services Strategy: Develop just enough architecture on demand to support digital effort's operations tempo. Provided in an enabling-consulting fashion.
    • TOGAF Principles mapped to DPBoK Contexts.
    • Enterprise Architecture Services mapped to DPBoK Contexts (EA Service Emergence Model).
  • Digital Technology Adoption
    • TOGAF Series Guide: Digital Technology Adoption: A Guide to Readiness Assessment and Roadmap Development.
    • Provides Digital Transformation Readiness Assessment (DTRA) technique. Assesses enterprise readiness for digital technology changes.
    • Factors Impacting Adoption (Categorized): Foundational, Impact, Sustaining. Examples: Vision, Sponsorship, IT Capability, Culture, Business Model Adaptability, Skills, Technology Maturity, Policy/Regulations, Funding, Governance, Value Realization.
    • Roadmap Process:
      • Step 1: Select the Adoption Approach (e.g., ABCD framework).
      • Step 2: Perform the DTRA (using assessment toolkit).
      • Step 3: Identify Factors Needing Management Attention (analyze output, understand implications of missing factors).
      • Step 4: Address Shortcomings and Initiate Adoption (plug gaps, progressive/Agile approach recommended for lower readiness types).
